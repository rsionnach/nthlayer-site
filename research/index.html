The thesis

The observability industry has converged on a single strategy for AI: making AI better at investigating incidents. Every vendor has an MCP integration. Every vendor has a chatbot. Every vendor has a demo where the AI finds root cause in thirty seconds.

Almost nobody is asking the other question: are the AI agents themselves making good decisions?

This research examines 9 established observability vendors, 14 AI-specific startups, 6 governance platforms, the major open-source standards, analyst reports from Gartner and Forrester, and conference talks from SREcon, QCon, and KubeCon. The finding is consistent: every tool monitors the mechanics of AI (latency, tokens, cost, error rates) while decision quality — reversal rates, confidence calibration, high-confidence failure rates — remains unmeasured in production.

The gap is not subtle. It is explicitly acknowledged by the people building and operating frontier AI systems.

The incident correlation landscape is particularly revealing. Datadog, New Relic, Splunk, Elastic, and PagerDuty all use runtime ML-based correlation — pattern matching on alert streams with no declared dependency context. Dynatrace comes closest with its auto-discovered Smartscape topology combined with Davis causal AI, but this topology is discovered, not declared. No vendor supports walking a declared dependency graph to disentangle concurrent AI-related incidents.

Two fundamental conflations

"AI for Ops" vs. "Ops for AI"

Thoughtworks articulated a distinction that the market is consistently failing to make: "AI for Ops" is about using AI to optimise IT operations. "Ops for AI" is about making AI systems themselves trustworthy, safe, and reliable. They warn that collapsing these two disciplines into a single definition "erases the nuance and risks applying the wrong playbook to the wrong problem."

Every established vendor is primarily investing in AI for Ops — using AI to help humans investigate incidents faster. Almost nobody is building Ops for AI — measuring whether AI systems themselves are reliable.

Governance vs. reliability

The AI agent governance space (Microsoft Agent Governance Toolkit, CortexHub, AControlLayer, Kore.ai, Acuvity, Aegis) focuses exclusively on pre-execution authorisation: is this agent allowed to do this? None of them systematically measure post-execution decision quality: did this agent make a good decision?

JetBrains put it clearly: "Governance frameworks define permissions, approval gates, and high-level constraints. They rarely account for how an agent interprets context, chains tools, or explores edge cases. The result is a structural gap."

The gap between governance and reliability is the space between the bouncer checking your ID at the door and the auditor reviewing whether the decisions made inside were sound. The industry has the bouncer. It doesn't have the auditor.

No open standard supports judgment SLOs

OpenTelemetry has merged evaluation event conventions (v1.39.0) with attributes for generic evaluation scores. The agentic systems proposal (Issue #2664) covers tasks, actions, agents, teams, artifacts, and memory — but no decision quality semantics. No gen_ai.decision.* or gen_ai.override.* attributes.

OpenSLO v1.0 supports traditional ratio-based SLIs. It has no concept of judgment quality, observation periods, confidence-weighted metrics, or human override signals.

All three major SLO frameworks (Sloth, Pyrra, Google SLO Generator) generate Prometheus rules exclusively for availability and latency SLOs. None support AI decision quality metrics.

OpenSRM is the only open specification that models AI decision quality as an SLO, with constructs for reversal rate, observation periods, high-confidence failure rate, and confidence thresholds.

Compound probability

The compound failure math is unforgiving. An AI agent with 85% per-step accuracy succeeds only about 20% of the time on a 10-step task. As organisations run dozens or hundreds of agents that interact with each other and with traditional services, individual agent monitoring cannot detect compound failures that emerge from interactions between agents.

"It's easier to write software, so we write much more of it, so the complexity goes up and not down, which means things break in more interesting ways, which means more incidents, more on call... all the improvements in the tooling will be cancelled by this ever-growing complexity." — Alex Palcuie, Anthropic, QCon London 2026

Only correlation across a declared dependency graph — not pattern matching from symptoms — can disentangle concurrent incidents in systems of this complexity.

The structural gap

The research identifies a market that is simultaneously booming and structurally incomplete. Billions flow into observability tooling, 40% of enterprise applications will integrate AI agents by end of 2026, and every major vendor has shipped an AI monitoring product. Yet the fundamental question — is this AI agent making good decisions? — remains unanswered by any shipping product.

Three findings stand out:

First, Anthropic's own SRE publicly stating that Claude "gets wrong correlation versus causation" validates that even frontier AI labs cannot trust their own models' judgment — and have no way to measure the failure rate systematically.

Second, the LangChain survey showing 89% have observability while quality remains the #1 barrier proves the gap is not an observability gap in the traditional sense — it's a decision quality measurement gap.

Third, Gartner's declaration that "even security observability" is insufficient provides the analyst authority to frame this as an entirely new category, not an incremental improvement on existing tools.

None of the gaps identified are temporary oversights that vendors will imminently fix. The architectural choices are structural. Established vendors built around metrics, logs, and traces for infrastructure. Startups built around SDK instrumentation for individual model evaluation. Governance platforms built around pre-execution policy gates.
